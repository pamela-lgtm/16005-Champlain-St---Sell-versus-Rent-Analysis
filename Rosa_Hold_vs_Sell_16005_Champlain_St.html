The Tax Reality — What Selling Now Could Cost You in Capital Gains
⚠ This is the conversation your realtor is unlikely to have with you
Capital gains tax is one of the most significant — and most overlooked — costs of selling a property. Your realtor's CMA will show you sale price projections. It will not show you what the IRS takes from that number. Before you decide to sell, you need to understand this clearly.
Capital Gains — The Key Facts for Your Investment Property
🔴 No Primary Residence Exclusion — You Owe on the Full Gain
Because this property has always been an investment — not your primary residence — there is no Section 121 exclusion available to shield your profit from capital gains tax. When you sell, you will owe federal capital gains tax on your full net gain. The rate you pay depends directly on your overall taxable income for that year — and it is not a small number.
⚠ Capital Gains Rates — Based on Your Income
Long-term capital gains rates (property held more than 1 year) are tiered by income:

0% — Taxable income below ~$47,000 (single) / ~$94,000 (married)
15% — Taxable income up to ~$518,000 (single) / ~$583,000 (married) — most investors fall here
20% — Taxable income above those thresholds

Additionally, if your income exceeds $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married), a 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) applies on top of your capital gains rate — bringing the effective rate to as high as 23.8% for higher earners.
🔴 Depreciation Recapture — A Flat 25% Tax You Cannot Avoid
If you have been claiming depreciation deductions on this investment property — which you should have been, as it is a significant tax benefit — the IRS will "recapture" that accumulated depreciation when you sell and tax it at a flat 25%, regardless of your income bracket. On a property depreciated over several years, this can add thousands of dollars to your tax bill on top of the capital gains rate. This is unavoidable and is separate from the capital gains calculation.
⚠ What This Means in Real Numbers
Using an illustrative example — property purchased for $300,000, sold today for $450,000:

Net gain: ~$150,000
Federal capital gains tax at 15%: ~$22,500
Depreciation recapture at 25% (on accumulated depreciation): additional thousands
NIIT at 3.8% (if applicable): ~$5,700
Estimated total federal tax exposure: $28,000–$45,000+ depending on income and years of depreciation claimed

None of these numbers appear on a CMA. They are real costs that reduce what you actually net from a sale — and they do not go away whether you sell now or sell later. The question is whether you sell now in a soft market and absorb both the tax burden and the market-driven concessions simultaneously, or whether you wait for better conditions and only deal with the tax when the sale price justifies it.
⚠ Important — Consult a Tax Professional
Capital gains rules are complex and depend on your specific purchase price, filing status, income level, and how long you have owned and occupied the property. The numbers above are illustrative. We strongly recommend speaking with a CPA or tax advisor before making your decision. We are not tax advisors, but we know enough to tell you that this conversation needs to happen before you sign a listing agreement.
